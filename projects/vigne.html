Vigne — Green Kitchen Marketplace

A curated e-commerce platform for sustainable, eco-friendly kitchen products. Built to convert conscious shoppers through storytelling, product education, and a checkout experience that is as clean as the products it sells.

The Challenge

Selling Sustainability Without Preaching It

The brief was nuanced: build an e-commerce site for eco-friendly kitchenware that didn't feel preachy or clinical. Most "green" brands make sustainability the entire personality — which alienates the majority of shoppers who just want a good product. Vigne had to be a great kitchen marketplace first, with sustainability as a natural quality badge rather than the entire pitch.

The technical challenge was building a performant, SEO-optimised storefront on a tight timeline with product filtering, a cart system, and an image-heavy catalogue that still loaded fast on mobile.

How It Was Built

Technical Decisions

Image Optimisation Strategy

Product photography is the lifeblood of e-commerce. Every image goes through Next.js next/image — automatic WebP conversion, lazy loading, and responsive srcset generation. The result: image-heavy pages that still score 90+ on Lighthouse mobile.

Cart with Zustand

Cart state lives in Zustand — persisted to localStorage so it survives page refreshes. The store is cleanly separated from UI, making it trivial to wire up to any checkout provider later. No prop drilling, no Redux overhead.

Product Filtering

Filters are URL-param driven — category, material, price range, eco-certification. This makes filtered pages shareable and indexable by search engines. The filter logic runs on the server (Next.js server components) so the initial render arrives fully filtered, with no loading flash.

Sanity CMS Integration

Non-technical staff manage product listings, blog posts, and homepage content through Sanity Studio. Type-safe queries via the Sanity client ensure the frontend never receives malformed data from a content edit.

Eco-Badge System

Products carry structured metadata tags (FSC-certified, BPA-free, carbon-neutral shipping). The badge rendering component is driven purely by this metadata — adding a new certification type requires zero code changes, just a new Sanity schema entry.

SEO Architecture

Category pages, product pages, and blog posts all have dynamically generated meta tags, structured data (JSON-LD Product schema), and sitemap entries — generated at build time from the CMS. Organic search gets the full picture of every product.

Key insight: E-commerce performance is conversion rate. Every 100ms of load time is a percentage point of lost sales. The entire architecture — SSG for product pages, streaming for cart, lazy load for images — is designed around that single truth.
AI-Assisted Development

How AI Helped

Building an e-commerce storefront involves a lot of predictable patterns — product cards, filter logic, cart management. AI handled the pattern work so I could focus on the differentiating details.

1

Zustand Store Design

I described the cart requirements — add, remove, update quantity, persist to localStorage, derive total — and Claude generated a clean, typed Zustand store. I reviewed and added the optimistic update for quantity changes. 15 minutes vs. an hour writing from scratch.

2

Sanity Schema Generation

Sanity schema definitions are verbose. I described each content type in plain English and had Claude generate the initial schema files. I reviewed for correctness, added validation rules, and had the CMS running in a fraction of the usual setup time.

3

Product Copy Refinement

Sample product descriptions were provided as bullet points. Claude transformed them into narrative copy that balanced warmth, sustainability messaging, and practical detail. I reviewed and adjusted the tone — the AI got to 70%, I handled the final 30%.

4

Accessibility Audit Assist

Before launch, I pasted component markup into Claude and asked it to identify WCAG 2.1 AA issues. It caught two missing aria-labels on icon buttons and a colour contrast failure on a secondary CTA. These would have been difficult to catch without a dedicated accessibility tool.
